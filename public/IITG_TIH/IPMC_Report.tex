\section*{Abstract}

Ionic Polymer-Metal Composites (IPMCs) are electroactive polymer actuators capable of generating large bending deformation under low-voltage electrical excitation. Due to their lightweight structure, flexibility, biomimetic characteristics, and low power consumption, IPMCs have attracted significant attention for applications in soft robotics, biomedical systems, underwater propulsion, artificial muscles, and adaptive actuation mechanisms.

This work presents a comprehensive experimental and machine learning-based investigation of IPMC actuation dynamics under varying voltage amplitudes and environmental conditions. An ESP32-CAM-based image acquisition system was developed for real-time deformation monitoring of cantilever-configured IPMC actuators subjected to alternating electrical excitation.

Experiments were conducted under WATER, AIR, and ACETONE(1\%) environments using excitation voltages ranging from 2.1 V to 4.9 V. Sequential image frames were recorded at 200 ms intervals during both stimulus and relaxation phases.

A computer vision-based image-processing framework was implemented for automated deformation-angle extraction using contour analysis, skeletonization, and vector-based angle estimation techniques.

The experimentally generated dataset contained 840 deformation observations across multiple operating conditions.

Experimental observations demonstrated strong voltage-dependent and environment-dependent actuation behavior. WATER exhibited the highest bending deformation due to enhanced ionic mobility and improved membrane hydration characteristics.

A deep neural network-based regression framework was developed to predict IPMC deformation under varying experimental conditions. The developed model achieved strong predictive performance with an $R^2$ score of 0.959, demonstrating excellent agreement between experimentally measured and model-predicted deformation behavior.

The developed framework establishes a complete methodology for experimental characterization, image-based deformation analysis, machine learning-driven predictive modeling, and intelligent actuation analysis of IPMC systems.

\section{Introduction}

Electroactive polymers (EAPs) are a class of smart materials capable of converting electrical energy into mechanical deformation. Among various electroactive materials, Ionic Polymer-Metal Composites (IPMCs) have emerged as highly promising soft actuators due to their low operating voltage, lightweight structure, flexibility, silent operation, and biomimetic actuation behavior.

An IPMC actuator typically consists of an ion-exchange polymer membrane coated with conductive metallic electrodes on both surfaces. When subjected to an electric field, hydrated ions migrate within the polymer matrix, resulting in internal stress gradients and macroscopic bending deformation.

Due to these unique electromechanical characteristics, IPMCs have attracted significant research interest in fields such as:

\begin{itemize}

\item soft robotics,

\item underwater propulsion systems,

\item biomedical microdevices,

\item artificial muscles,

\item flexible sensors,

\item adaptive actuation systems.

\end{itemize}

Despite these advantages, accurate prediction of IPMC deformation remains challenging due to strongly coupled nonlinear electrochemical, hydraulic, and mechanical transport phenomena.

The deformation behavior is influenced by multiple interacting factors including:

\begin{itemize}

\item applied voltage,

\item membrane hydration,

\item ionic mobility,

\item environmental conditions,

\item transient relaxation dynamics,

\item solvent transport mechanisms.

\end{itemize}

Conventional analytical modeling approaches often require complex multiphysics formulations and significant computational resources.

Recent advances in machine learning and data-driven modeling have enabled development of predictive frameworks capable of learning nonlinear deformation relationships directly from experimental observations.

The present work focuses on experimental characterization and deep learning-based predictive modeling of IPMC deformation behavior under varying voltage amplitudes and environmental media.

The overall objective of the work is to establish an integrated framework combining:

\begin{itemize}

\item experimental actuation analysis,

\item image-based deformation extraction,

\item dataset generation,

\item deep neural network-based deformation prediction.

\end{itemize}

\section{Literature Background}

Extensive research has been conducted on the electromechanical behavior and modeling of Ionic Polymer-Metal Composite actuators.

Shahinpoor and Kim established foundational theoretical understanding of ion transport and electroactive deformation mechanisms in IPMC systems.

Bar-Cohen demonstrated the applicability of electroactive polymers in biomimetic robotics and artificial muscle systems.

Several studies have focused on multiphysics modeling of IPMC actuation using electrochemical transport equations, viscoelastic beam theory, and coupled ionic diffusion models.

However, physics-based approaches often become computationally expensive under dynamically varying environmental conditions.

Recent developments in machine learning and deep learning have enabled data-driven prediction of nonlinear actuator behavior directly from experimentally generated datasets.

Deep neural networks have demonstrated strong capability in nonlinear regression, transient system identification, and intelligent soft-actuator modeling.

The present work extends these developments by integrating experimental deformation analysis with deep neural network-based predictive modeling under multiple environmental conditions.

\section{Working Principle of IPMC}

Ionic Polymer-Metal Composites (IPMCs) operate based on ion migration and electro-osmotic transport within an ion-exchange polymer membrane.

An IPMC actuator generally consists of:

\begin{itemize}

\item a Nafion-based ion-exchange polymer membrane,

\item conductive metallic electrodes coated on both surfaces,

\item hydrated mobile cations within the polymer matrix.

\end{itemize}

When an electric field is applied across the electrodes, hydrated cations migrate toward the cathode side of the actuator.

The migration of hydrated ions causes differential swelling inside the polymer structure due to uneven water redistribution.

As a result, one side of the actuator expands while the opposite side contracts, producing macroscopic bending deformation.

The electromechanical behavior of IPMCs is strongly dependent on:

\begin{itemize}

\item ionic diffusivity,

\item membrane hydration,

\item dielectric properties,

\item elastic modulus,

\item environmental medium,

\item excitation voltage and frequency.

\end{itemize}

The deformation process is nonlinear and time-dependent due to coupled electrochemical, hydraulic, and mechanical interactions inside the polymer matrix.

Back-relaxation behavior may also occur due to redistribution of ions and internal pressure gradients after initial deformation.

\section{Experimental Methodology}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

The experimental setup consisted of an IPMC actuator connected to a controlled AC voltage excitation source capable of delivering low-voltage electrical stimulation.

The actuator was fixed in a cantilever configuration, with one end rigidly constrained and the opposite end free to deform under electrical excitation.

A camera-based acquisition system was developed using an ESP32-CAM module for real-time image acquisition and deformation tracking.

The ESP32-CAM module was positioned perpendicular to the plane of deformation to minimize perspective distortion during image capture.

The complete acquisition system was programmed using the Arduino IDE platform.

Sequential image frames were captured at a temporal interval of 200 ms, corresponding to a sampling frequency of approximately 5 Hz.

\subsection{AC Voltage Excitation Strategy}

AC excitation was selected to generate periodic bidirectional deformation of the IPMC actuator.

Under alternating electric fields, hydrated ions continuously redistribute between the electrodes, producing oscillatory bending behavior.

Compared to constant DC excitation, AC excitation reduces prolonged ion accumulation near the electrodes and helps mitigate excessive electrolysis effects.

The applied AC waveform also enabled observation of transient deformation dynamics during repeated stimulus-relaxation cycles.

The resulting deformation profiles exhibited transient nonlinear actuation behavior under alternating electrical excitation.

\begin{equation}
v(t) = V_0 \sin(2\pi f t)
\end{equation}

where:

\begin{itemize}

\item $V_0$ represents voltage amplitude

\item $f$ represents excitation frequency

\item $t$ represents time

\end{itemize}

Experiments were conducted under three environmental conditions:

\begin{itemize}

\item WATER

\item AIR

\item ACETONE(1\%)

\end{itemize}

Voltage amplitudes ranging from 2.1 V to 4.9 V were applied sequentially during experimentation.

\section{Image Processing and Deformation Extraction}

To quantify IPMC deformation, the recorded image sequences were processed using a computer vision-based deformation analysis pipeline.

The processing workflow involved:

\begin{itemize}

\item grayscale conversion,

\item Gaussian smoothing,

\item adaptive thresholding,

\item contour detection,

\item skeletonization,

\item centerline estimation,

\item tip tracking,

\item bending-angle estimation.

\end{itemize}

The deformation angle was computed relative to the undeformed reference orientation using vector-angle estimation.

\begin{equation}
\theta = \arccos\left(\frac{\vec{r} \cdot \vec{d}}{\|\vec{r}\|\,\|\vec{d}\|}\right)
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
\Delta \theta = \theta_t - \theta_{t-1}
\end{equation}

where $\Delta \theta$ represents the temporal deformation derivative.

\vspace{0.3cm}

where:

\begin{itemize}

\item $\vec{r}$ represents the reference orientation vector

\item $\vec{d}$ represents the deformed tangent vector

\item $\theta$ represents the instantaneous bending angle

\end{itemize}

\subsection{Back-Relaxation Phenomenon}

One of the characteristic behaviors of IPMC actuators is back-relaxation.

Back-relaxation refers to the gradual reduction in bending displacement after the actuator initially reaches peak deformation under continuous electrical excitation.

This phenomenon occurs due to redistribution of hydrated ions and internal pressure equalization within the polymer membrane.

The transient response of the actuator is governed by competing electrochemical and hydraulic transport mechanisms.

Experimental studies reported in literature indicate that parameters such as ionic diffusivity, dielectric constant, hydraulic permeability, and hydration level significantly influence the magnitude of back-relaxation behavior.

Environmental conditions also affect this phenomenon due to changes in water retention and ion mobility within the polymer matrix.

\subsection{Effect of Environmental Media}

The surrounding medium significantly influenced the actuation behavior of the IPMC actuator.

Among all investigated media, WATER demonstrated the highest deformation response.

This behavior can be attributed to improved membrane hydration and enhanced ionic mobility within the polymer structure.

Higher water content increases ion transport efficiency and electro-osmotic flow, resulting in larger bending displacement.

In AIR, gradual dehydration of the membrane reduces ionic conductivity and suppresses deformation performance.

ACETONE(1\%) introduced additional transport disturbances and partial reduction in effective hydration, leading to comparatively lower bending amplitudes.

These observations are consistent with previously reported studies on hydration-dependent IPMC actuation behavior.

\section{Machine Learning Framework}

\subsection{Feature Engineering and Data Preprocessing}

Prior to neural-network development, the experimentally generated deformation dataset was preprocessed and transformed into a structured regression-learning framework.

The input parameters utilized for predictive modeling included:

\begin{itemize}

\item applied voltage amplitude,

\item time information,

\item environmental medium,

\item actuation phase.

\end{itemize}

Categorical variables such as environmental medium and phase identifiers were converted into numerical format using one-hot encoding techniques.

Feature standardization was subsequently performed using z-score normalization to improve convergence stability and training efficiency of the neural network.

The target output variable was defined as the experimentally extracted bending deformation angle.

The complete dataset containing 840 observations was divided into training and testing subsets using an 80:20 split ratio.

The machine learning framework was implemented using TensorFlow, Scikit-learn, NumPy, and Pandas libraries within the Google Colaboratory environment.

\subsection{Motivation for Data-Driven Modeling}

The electromechanical deformation behavior of Ionic Polymer-Metal Composite (IPMC) actuators is governed by highly coupled nonlinear electrochemical, hydraulic, and mechanical interactions.

Conventional analytical modeling approaches require complex multiphysics formulations involving ionic transport equations, electro-osmotic flow behavior, viscoelastic deformation, and transient hydration dynamics.

These formulations become computationally intensive and difficult to generalize under varying environmental conditions.

Machine learning provides an alternative data-driven framework capable of learning nonlinear relationships directly from experimentally generated deformation sequences.

By utilizing experimentally extracted deformation data, neural-network-based models can approximate the nonlinear mapping between operating parameters and resulting deformation response without explicitly solving coupled governing equations.

Such approaches are highly relevant for future soft robotic systems, intelligent actuators, and real-time embedded control applications.

\subsection{Neural Network Architecture}

A feedforward deep neural network (DNN) regression architecture was implemented for predictive modeling of IPMC deformation behavior.

The neural-network model utilized the following input parameters:

\begin{itemize}

\item applied voltage,

\item temporal information,

\item environmental medium,

\item actuation phase.

\end{itemize}

The implemented architecture consisted of multiple fully connected dense layers with nonlinear activation functions.

Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU) activation functions were utilized to improve nonlinear feature learning capability and computational efficiency.

Dropout regularization layers were incorporated to reduce overfitting and improve model generalization performance.

The developed architecture consisted of:

\begin{itemize}

\item Input Layer

\item Dense Layer (128 neurons, ReLU)

\item Dropout Layer (20\%)

\item Dense Layer (64 neurons, ReLU)

\item Dropout Layer (20\%)

\item Dense Layer (32 neurons, ReLU)

\item Output Regression Layer

\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:nn_visual} presents the structural layout of the implemented feedforward neural-network regression model.

The input feature vector contains experimentally extracted operating parameters such as voltage amplitude, temporal information, environmental medium encoding, and actuation phase information. These features are passed through successive fully connected dense layers, where nonlinear ReLU activation functions enable the network to learn complex relationships between experimental conditions and bending deformation response.

Dropout layers are placed between dense layers to randomly deactivate a fraction of neurons during training, thereby reducing overfitting and improving generalization for the limited experimental dataset. The final output layer contains a single regression node that predicts the instantaneous IPMC bending angle.

\begin{figure}[H]

\centering

\safeimage{0.9\linewidth}{images/NN_VISUAL.png}

\caption{Feedforward neural-network architecture used for IPMC deformation-angle regression.}

\label{fig:nn_visual}

\end{figure}

The model was trained using the Adam optimization algorithm with Mean Squared Error (MSE) as the objective loss function.

Early stopping criteria were implemented during training to prevent excessive overfitting and improve convergence stability.

\section{Limitations of Present Work}

The present work primarily focused on low-voltage deformation analysis under controlled laboratory conditions.

Several factors influencing IPMC behavior were not explicitly investigated, including:

\begin{itemize}

\item excitation-frequency dependency,

\item long-term membrane degradation,

\item electrode aging,

\item temperature-dependent ionic transport,

\item humidity-controlled environmental effects,

\item membrane-thickness variation,

\item hysteresis characterization.

\end{itemize}

Additionally, the developed neural network model was trained using experimentally generated data within a limited operating range.

Future studies involving larger datasets, additional environmental conditions, and real-time embedded implementation can further improve predictive accuracy and generalization capability.

This work presented a comprehensive experimental and machine learning-based investigation of Ionic Polymer-Metal Composite (IPMC) actuation dynamics under varying voltage amplitudes and environmental conditions.

An ESP32-CAM-based embedded imaging system was successfully developed for real-time deformation monitoring of cantilever-configured IPMC actuators.

A computer vision-based image-processing framework was implemented for automated extraction of bending deformation angles from experimentally captured image sequences.

Experimental investigations demonstrated strong voltage-dependent and environment-dependent actuation behavior.

Among all investigated environmental conditions, WATER exhibited the highest deformation response due to enhanced membrane hydration and improved ionic mobility.

The experimentally generated dataset consisting of 840 observations was subsequently utilized for deep learning-based predictive modeling.

A deep neural network regression framework was successfully developed for prediction of nonlinear IPMC deformation behavior.

The developed model demonstrated strong predictive performance with low regression error and an $R^2$ score of 0.959, indicating excellent agreement between experimentally measured and model-predicted deformation.

Residual analysis and convergence behavior further confirmed stable generalization capability of the proposed framework.

The overall methodology establishes an integrated framework for experimental characterization, intelligent deformation analysis, and machine learning-driven predictive modeling of soft electroactive polymer systems.

The developed framework can contribute toward future development of intelligent soft robotic actuators, adaptive control systems, and AI-assisted smart-material platforms.
